The Concept

The brand goes
where the people are.

The Iron & Embers food truck is a fully branded, coal-mining-heritage mobile kitchen serving a focused menu of all-day breakfast and handhelds — the same menu items that anchor Phase 2 and Phase 3. The brand is consistent from day one.

The truck operates Tuesday through Sunday with a weekly route covering high-traffic locations across Crawford County and the Four-State region. Event and catering bookings are layered on top and represent the highest-margin revenue channel in the model.

Every guest who discovers Iron & Embers at a festival or a brewery parking lot is a pre-sold customer for every phase that follows. The truck is a moving billboard, a brand validator, and a revenue generator simultaneously.

The Build Decision

Two paths to the truck.
Both proven. Both viable.

The truck is the largest single capital decision in Phase 1. Two paths exist — buying used and converting, or buying new/custom. The right choice depends on timeline, budget, and risk tolerance. Total costs are closer than most people expect. The recommendation is clear.

Path A · Recommended

Used Truck + Conversion

$65,000 – $85,000 total

Ready in 10–14 weeks

Buy a used step van, cargo van, or purpose-built used food truck from the secondary market. A solid unit in the $40,000–$55,000 range typically comes with functional cooking equipment, a generator, and basic plumbing. Wrap it in Coal Black with Iron & Embers branding, upgrade what's needed, and go.

The Commissary is the Critical Path

Kansas requires all mobile food units to operate from a licensed commissary kitchen. This is where food is prepped, water is filled, gray water is disposed, and the truck is cleaned. The home kitchen does not qualify. The commissary agreement must be in hand before the KDA license application can be submitted — resolve this first, before anything else.

No Statewide Reciprocity

Kansas has no statewide reciprocity for mobile food vendors. Each city requires its own permit with 30–60 day lead times. Submit all city applications simultaneously at the start of the permitting phase — not sequentially.

Sales Tax on Prepared Food

Prepared food sold from a food truck is fully taxable at the Kansas state rate (6.5%) plus local rate — even though raw groceries are now 0%. Collect destination-based tax at the address of each sale.

Industry Tailwinds

The U.S. food truck industry reached an estimated $2.8 billion in revenue in 2025, growing at a 13.2% five-year CAGR from 2020 to 2025. There are now over 92,000 food truck businesses nationally. The average U.S. food truck generates $346,000 in annual revenue with profit margins of 6.2–6.8% — higher than traditional restaurants at 1–3%.

The South led with a 35.8% revenue share of the national market in 2025. Corporate catering demand is a structural tailwind: 53% of corporate buyers plan to increase catering budgets, and 80% order catering at least once a month.

Why Phase 1 First

Six reasons the truck
comes before the building.

01

Lowest Capital Risk

$65K–$115K vs. $485K–$550K for the full venue. If the concept needs refinement, it costs a fraction of what a full build-out would to find out.

02

No Lease Obligation

The truck goes where demand is. No long-term lease means no bad-location risk. If a spot doesn't work, you move. You can't do that with a building.

03

No Liquor License Wait

The most time-consuming regulatory hurdle in Phases 2 and 3 doesn't exist in Phase 1. Food service only — permits can be navigated in weeks, not months.

04

Real Market Proof

Proves the menu with real customers before committing to a full commercial kitchen buildout. Identifies what sells, what doesn't, and what the market actually wants.

05

Funds What Follows

Generates cash flow during the 12–24 month planning period for Phase 2. An operating truck with documented revenue is materially more compelling to an SBA lender than a proposal alone.

06

Builds the Audience

Social following, email list, loyal repeat customers, and community relationships — all inherited by Phase 2 on opening day. The truck does the marketing for everything that comes next.

Risk Analysis

We've mapped the risks.
Here's how we respond.

RiskLikelihoodMitigation
Mechanical breakdown Medium Pre-purchase inspection mandatory. $2,000–$3,000 maintenance reserve. Mobile mechanic relationship established before launch. Used truck with functional equipment reduces this vs. aging vehicles.
Slow initial ramp-up Medium 3-month working capital reserve. Owner-operated in Year 1 minimizes fixed labor cost during ramp. Catering bookings confirmed before launch provide early revenue floor.
Commissary availability Low–Med Identify commissary as the first action item — before truck purchase, before licensing. Secure written agreement 60+ days before target launch. Multiple options in Crawford County.
Kansas city permit delays Medium Submit all city permit applications simultaneously, not sequentially. Start with Pittsburg only if needed and expand territory as additional permits arrive.
Weather impact (winter seasons) Seasonal Shift to catering and brewery/indoor venue partnerships November–March. Holiday corporate catering push (Nov–Dec) is the highest-revenue window of the year. Winter does not mean closure.
Food cost volatility Medium Focused menu limits exposure. Weekly food cost tracking. Menu engineering if any item exceeds 35% food cost. Breakfast items carry the best margins in the lineup.
Competitive response Low No branded competitor in Southeast Kansas mobile food market at this level. Iron & Embers identity is a durable differentiator — the heritage narrative, wrap, and menu theming cannot be quickly replicated.
